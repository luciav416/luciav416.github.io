The solitude of violence not only destroys bodies but also severs the bonds that once united the community. The plaza, which should be a place of gathering, turns into a battlefield. This is where the isolation of the Latin American soul begins.

"Colonel Aureliano Buendía promoted thirty-two armed uprisings and lost them all. (...) He looked out the window and saw the deserted square, with dust stirred by the wind."
— One Hundred Years of Solitude, Gabriel García Márquez.

Death comes quietly to the countryside, and with it, solitude. There is no comfort in the eyes of others, only the certainty that, in the end, each one faces the darkness alone.

"In reality, someone's death is the only completely irreversible event, the only one. And yet, even from death one sometimes returns, by ways that are not comprehensible."
— Casa tomada, Julio Cortázar.

The game has lost its joy. In the eyes of the children and the men, only the weight of passing days remains, unchanged, without hope. Solitude is felt, even in company.

"We were left alone, and every now and then it was nice to hear the clinking of a cup in the dining room, the murmur of conversation reaching us as a muffled, drowned sound."
— Casa tomada, Julio Cortázar.

The distance between their bodies reflects the distance between their souls. In the countryside, the silence is deep, and solitude grows like weeds, intertwining hearts but separating bodies.

"He had seen so many wars and so many disasters, and in all of them he found the same solitude; not the solitude of being alone, but the inability to connect with anyone, not even those closest to him."
— One Hundred Years of Solitude, Gabriel García Márquez.

The solitude of two souls sharing the same roof, but not the same heart. On their faces, life has drawn the lines of a love that has vanished, yet was never fully expressed.

"There was no worse nostalgia
than longing for what never happened."
— Casa tomada, Julio Cortázar.

"Bogotá, January 18, 1932. My dear José Luis, I miss you so much, and it seems we have been forgotten, as I haven't received a letter or telegram; maybe the crickets and cockroaches have eaten the paper. To your dear father, whom I loved so much. When are you coming? We’ve been invited to Sebastopol on Saturday. Receive, with your father, many hugs and kisses from your mother who thinks of you a lot."

The Magdalena River carries memories and promises, but it never answers. The traveler's solitude is not the absence of company but the certainty that the river flows indifferently, as hopes fade away into its waters.

"The races condemned to one hundred years of solitude
did not have a second chance on earth."

— One Hundred Years of Solitude, Gabriel García Márquez.
